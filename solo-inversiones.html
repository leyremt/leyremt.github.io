Personal Project

Solo Inversiones

It reads our group chat and turns the stock talk into a list that prices itself and stays current.

LLM extraction Claude Haiku Serverless Automation Personal project
Stack: Python · Anthropic API (Claude Haiku) · GitHub Actions (scheduled) · Yahoo Finance API · Netlify (static hosting)

Overview

Every day it reads our investing chat and uses an LLM to pull out every company someone mentioned — the ticker, the market, the theme, and why it came up. Then it attaches a fresh price to each and pushes the whole thing to a static site that redeploys itself. Nothing to run, nothing to maintain — the bill comes to a few cents a month.

The problem

The chat was never only about markets. A good stock idea sat in the same thread as football, memes and whatever we were doing that weekend, and within a day it had scrolled out of sight. A week later someone would ask why we'd even looked at a name, and nobody could remember. Everything we needed was buried in the history — it just wasn't searchable, and the reasoning behind each name was gone.

The idea

Asking people to log tickers by hand was never going to work. Nobody does that in a group chat, and I wasn't going to either. So the bot does the remembering instead. It catches every mention and keeps the reason attached, then shows the lot on a dashboard that stays current by itself and costs nothing to run.

How it works

A bot picks up new messages from the group and passes them to an LLM (Claude Haiku), which pulls out the companies and structures each one: ticker, exchange, theme, and a line on why it came up. A daily job then adds the latest price and change to every company and writes the result to a static page that publishes itself. Scheduled runs and free hosting handle the rest — there's nothing to keep alive.

The hard part

Reading how people actually type

Real chat is messy on purpose. Someone writes Timofónica when they mean Telefónica, or Ibertrola for Iberdrola. Half of it is in-jokes, and the model still has to see through it.

"alguien sigue timofónica?" "ibertrola está regalada"
→
TEF · Telefónica IBE · Iberdrola

At temperature 0, fully deterministic, it read too literally — it couldn't get from the joke to the actual company. Nudging the temperature up a little gave it enough slack to make that jump and land on the right ticker, but not so much that it started inventing names nobody had said. I found that line by trying to break it, not by guessing.

Decisions & trade-offs

Cost & upkeep

Serverless, running on scheduled jobs and free static hosting. The cost of that is a once-a-day refresh instead of real time, which I was happy to pay — this is meant to remember what we discussed, not to trade the open, so daily is plenty. In return the bill stays at cents and there's nothing to maintain.

Extraction

Tuned the model for messy human input rather than clean tickers — that was the temperature call above. The interesting decisions in an LLM product usually aren't in the prompt; they're in how you deal with the inputs you don't control.

Privacy

Attribution is anonymized on purpose. The site says the group mentioned a company — it never says who. My friends' names don't leave the chat, and that was a rule from day one, not something I patched in later.

Simplicity

No framework, no dependencies, a plain static frontend. Fewer moving parts, fewer things that can break while I'm not looking. For something meant to run untouched for months, that mattered more than any feature.

What it delivers

Runs itself
Reads, extracts, prices, republishes — every day, without me.
Near-zero cost
A few cents a month, all in.
Structure from noise
Turns loose chat into structured data you can query: ticker, market, theme, context.
Name to signal
Go from a name in the chat to its price and what's moving it, in one step.
Survives real chat
Holds up against the typos and wordplay a real group throws at it.

What's next

Making each mention say more: sentiment (bullish or bearish), price targets when someone states one, and a record of how often — and how — a name keeps coming up. Less of a ticker list, more of a small research feed with a point of view.

Why it matters

Small project, built on my own from end to end — but it's a fair picture of how I work: find what actually annoys people, build the smallest thing that fixes it, and be honest about what you're trading away to get there. The parts that are genuinely hard, I'd rather settle by testing than by assuming I'm right.
